# ICP snapshot: who we're actually selling to

Three people sit on every deal that matters, and none of them wake up thinking about "learning platforms." They think about headcount plans, audit season, and who gets blamed for the last vendor that didn't work out.

## The economic buyer: CHRO / VP People / L&D Director

A 200-2,000 employee company, usually mid-reorg or mid-compliance-cycle. Signs the deal, defends the line item at the next leadership review, and spends most days on headcount planning with the CFO, one eye on attrition and the HR renewal up this quarter.

Judged on: completion percentages that hold up in an audit, turnover in the roles L&D is meant to protect, a board-ready number that isn't "we ran 40 workshops this year."

Burned by: a platform that reported 94% completion, then lost three key hires eight months later - nobody could explain "trained" versus "capable," and it was their name on the renewal.

Leans in when: a number would survive being read aloud in a leadership meeting. Not a dashboard, a sentence.

## The champion: L&D Manager / People Ops Lead

Daily reality: chasing completion rates for mandatory modules, rebuilding the same reporting deck by hand every month. Judged on adoption and completion rates, and a clean audit trail when Legal or a certifying body asks.

Burned by: launching a platform with real enthusiasm, watching usage fall off a cliff by month two, explaining the flatline to their own manager more than once.

Leans in when: something takes a manual task off their plate for good, not a nicer-looking version of it.

## The blocker: IT/Security, Procurement, Works Council (DE)

For IT/Security: another vendor in the review queue behind ten others, judged on clean SSO and no data residency surprises down the line. For a German works council: whether a new system tracks individuals in ways employees never agreed to - a real veto point in Germany, not a formality.

Burned by: a vendor that said "certification in progress" and meant "maybe next year," or a rollout flagged mid-way because nobody looped in the works council early.

Leans in when: answers to standard questions are ready before they ask, and monitoring concerns are handled in the pitch, not a fire drill after signature.
