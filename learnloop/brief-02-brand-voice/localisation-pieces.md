# Localisation Pieces — EN source → DE / ES transcreation

> These are on-voice English pieces heading into the DACH + Spain launch. Your job: **transcreate** them, not translate them. A literal translation will carry the English rhythm and directness into a market where they land differently. Recreate the voice using the DE/ES guidance in `voice-guide.md`.
>
> Each piece flags **the trap** — the specific thing that breaks if you translate word-for-word. DE and ES versions are left blank on purpose.
>
> *(Fictional. See `../learnloop-company-bible.md`.)*

## Piece 1 — Blog headline + opening (the metaphor trap)

**EN source:**

> **A library isn't a loop.**
>
> Cognita will sell you 8,000 courses and a search bar. That's a library. You can walk in, take a book off the shelf, and walk out — and nobody ever checks whether you read it, understood it, or can do anything differently because of it.
>
> LearnLoop closes the loop: learn it, use it, prove it landed. The catalog is the easy part. The loop is the point.

**⚠️ The trap:** The whole piece rides on the "library vs. loop" wordplay and the product name *LearnLoop* / *Loop*. In German, "loop" (*Schleife* / *Kreislauf* / *Regelkreis*) doesn't carry the same neat contrast with "library" (*Bibliothek*), and the pun evaporates. In Spanish, *biblioteca* vs. *bucle/ciclo* has the same problem. **Don't translate the pun — find the market's own version of the contrast.** German may want a "*Katalog* vs. *Wirkung*" (catalog vs. effect) framing; Spanish may want "*estantería* vs. *resultado*." Keep the product name in English, but let the surrounding metaphor be native. The claim that must survive: a catalog proves nothing landed.

## Piece 2 — Landing-page hero (the bold-claim-needs-softening-for-DE trap)

**EN source:**

> **Completion is a vanity metric. Stop reporting it.**
>
> Your 90% completion rate looks great in the board deck and means nothing on the floor. LearnLoop tracks capability — whether the skill got used — so the number you report is the number that actually moved the business.
>
> **[Start your free trial]**

**⚠️ The trap:** "Stop reporting it" is a blunt imperative that works in confident English/NL/UK copy. In the **German market it reads as presumptuous** if it isn't immediately backed — a German L&D director accepts a provocative claim *after* the proof, not before. Keep the contrarian spine, but restructure so the substantiation comes fast (a mechanism or a number right behind the claim), and use Sie-Form throughout. For Spanish, the claim can stay bold but wants a warmer, more inclusive framing — "todos hemos presentado esa cifra" (we've all reported that number) rather than a flat command — so it lands as a shared truth, not an accusation. Note also: `90%` → `90 %` in DE (space before the sign).

## Piece 3 — Nurture email opening (the idiom + register trap)

**EN source:**

> **Subject: The training landed. Did the skill?**
>
> Quick one. Your team finished the onboarding path last month — nice work getting them through it. But "getting them through it" and "they can now do the job" are two different things, and only one of them shows up in your numbers.
>
> Here's the 3-minute version of how we close that gap.

**⚠️ The trap:** Two things break literally. First, **"the skill landed" / "did the skill land"** is an English idiom — a direct translation (*ist die Fähigkeit gelandet?* / *¿aterrizó la habilidad?*) is nonsense in both languages. Find the native equivalent for "did it actually stick / take hold" (DE something like *ist die Kompetenz wirklich angekommen?*; ES *¿se consolidó de verdad?*). Second, **"Quick one" and "nice work"** are casual English register. In DE this must move to Sie-Form and lose the backslap without going cold; in ES it can stay warm but should feel natural, not translated-casual. The subject line's punchy two-question rhythm is the hardest thing to preserve — recreate the *tension* between the two clauses, don't copy the structure.

### How to know you got it right

Back-translate your DE and ES versions to English. If the result still (1) leads with a claim, (2) names the real pain, and (3) proves instead of promises — the voice survived. If it came back sounding like any other learning vendor, you translated instead of transcreated. Try again.
