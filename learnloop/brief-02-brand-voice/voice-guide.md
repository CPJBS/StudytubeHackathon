# LearnLoop — Brand Voice Guide

> The standard the check-loop enforces. If a draft doesn't match this, it isn't ready.
> *(Fictional company. See `../learnloop-company-bible.md`.)*

---

## Who we're talking to

L&D leaders, People/HR directors, People Ops managers at mid-market companies (200–2,000 employees). They've bought training tools before. Some of those tools failed — nobody logged in, the completions were real but the skills weren't. They're smart, tired of vendor noise, and they can smell a pitch from the subject line.

**So we talk to them as peers, not prospects.** We assume they already know the obvious. We earn attention with a claim they haven't heard, a number they can't ignore, or a pain they've felt but nobody's named.

---

## The voice in one line

**Direct, confident, a little contrarian, human.** Short sentences. Proof over hype. We say the thing.

## The four principles

1. **Lead with a claim or a number.** Not a windup. The first sentence should be able to stand alone and still be worth reading. "Most learning tools track completions. We track whether the skill got used." — not "Learning is more important than ever before."

2. **Name the real pain.** Say the uncomfortable thing out loud. L&D leaders live with problems nobody in a sales deck will admit exist (training nobody uses, dashboards that lie, budget defended with completion rates). Naming it is how you earn the next sentence.

3. **Proof over adjectives.** A number, a mechanism, or a named outcome beats any modifier. "Powerful" is a promise. "Cut onboarding from 6 weeks to 4" is proof. If you can't back a claim, cut it — don't soften it.

4. **Talk like a person, warmly.** Confident isn't cold. Contractions are fine. A dry aside is fine. We're direct because we respect the reader's time, not because we're above them.

---

## DO

- Lead with the claim, the number, or the pain — sentence one.
- Write short. One idea per sentence. Vary length for rhythm, but default short.
- Use concrete nouns and specific numbers ("6 publishers," "0.4% reply rate," "8,000 courses").
- Say "you" and "we." Use contractions.
- Name competitors' failure modes as mechanisms, not insults ("a library, not a loop").
- End on a point, not a platitude.
- Let one dry, human aside through per piece. It signals a person wrote this.

## DON'T

**Banned words and phrases — never ship these:**
`empower` · `unlock` · `seamless` · `leverage` · `synergy` · `in today's world` / `in today's fast-paced world` · `drive results` · `solutions` (as a filler noun) · `game-changer` · `revolutionize` · `best-in-class` · `robust` · `cutting-edge` · `elevate` · `supercharge`

**Banned moves:**
- **Hedging.** No "can help you potentially improve," "may be able to," "we believe this could." Say it or cut it.
- **The windup.** No "In today's fast-paced business environment, organizations are increasingly…" Delete every sentence that only exists to arrive at the actual point.
- **Adjective stacking.** "A powerful, intuitive, comprehensive platform" says nothing. Pick the one true claim and prove it.
- **Feature soup.** Don't list what it has. Say what it changes.
- **Corporate-neutral tone.** If it could be any SaaS company's blog, it's failed.
- **AI-cadence.** No "It's not just X — it's Y." No "X. Full stop." No tricolon-for-the-sake-of-rhythm.

## Tone examples — generic → on-voice

### Example 1 — blog opener
**Generic:** "In today's fast-paced business world, organizations are increasingly looking for solutions that empower their teams and drive results through seamless learning experiences."

**On-voice:** "Your people finished the training. Can any of them do the job differently now? Most learning tools can't answer that. That's the whole problem."

*Why: the generic version is four banned phrases in one sentence and says nothing. The on-voice version leads with the reader's real, unanswered question — the pain — and lands the thesis in three short beats.*

---

### Example 2 — product line
**Generic:** "Our robust, best-in-class platform leverages powerful analytics to unlock the full potential of your workforce."

**On-voice:** "Loop maps who can actually do what, flags the gaps, and tells you which learning closes them. No adjectives — just the skills you have and the ones you don't."

*Why: the generic version stacks adjectives and banned words around zero mechanism. On-voice names the mechanism (map → flag → recommend) and even calls out its own restraint as an aside.*

---

### Example 3 — email nurture line
**Generic:** "We wanted to reach out and share how LearnLoop can help you potentially improve engagement and elevate your L&D strategy going forward."

**On-voice:** "Quick one: your last training had a 92% completion rate and — by your own admission on our call — changed nothing. Completion isn't the metric. Here's the one that is."

*Why: the generic version hedges twice ("can help," "potentially") and reaches out to say nothing. On-voice opens with a specific, slightly uncomfortable callback and reframes the metric — the contrarian move.*

---

### Example 4 — competitor line
**Generic:** "Unlike other solutions on the market, LearnLoop offers a comprehensive, integrated approach to learning and development."

**On-voice:** "Cognita gives you 8,000 courses and no idea if a single one landed. A library isn't a loop."

*Why: the generic version claims differentiation without naming it. On-voice names the competitor's actual failure mode as a mechanism and lands it with the brand's core metaphor.*

## Crossing languages — adapt, don't translate

The DACH and Spain launch is the real test. A literal translation of an English sentence carries the English *rhythm* and the English *directness level* into a market where they read differently. The voice has to be recreated, not converted. Same spine (lead with the claim, proof over hype, name the pain); different surface.

### German (DE) — DACH launch

- **Sie-Form, always.** Address the reader formally. `du` reads as either startup-cocky or consumer-app, and our buyer is a corporate L&D or HR director.
- **More precise, less punchy.** German rewards specificity and completeness over the clipped English rhythm. A three-word English sentence often needs to become a complete, precise German one. Don't chase the English staccato — it reads as underbaked.
- **Claims need harder proof.** The German market is more skeptical of a bold unbacked claim than the NL/UK reader. Keep the confidence; attach the number. A claim that flies in English ("Completion is a vanity metric") wants a source or a mechanism behind it in German.
- **Contrarian, but earned.** The contrarian edge stays — but back it immediately. German readers accept a provocative claim once it's substantiated, not before.
- **Formatting: space before the percent sign** (`92 %`), and watch compound nouns — precision beats cleverness.
- **What survives:** proof over hype, naming the real pain, no banned corporate-filler (there is German filler too — `ganzheitlich`, `nahtlos`, `maßgeschneiderte Lösungen` — ban those equivalents).

### Spanish (ES) — Spain launch

- **Warmer, more relational.** Spanish B2B copy carries more human warmth than clipped English without going soft. Lead with the claim, but the connective tissue between sentences can be a touch more personal.
- **Usted vs. tú:** default to a warm-professional register — Spanish B2B increasingly uses `tú` for approachability, but for an HR/L&D director audience, lean `usted` or a neutral impersonal construction. Pick one per market and hold it.
- **Rhythm can breathe.** The English short-sentence staccato can feel curt in Spanish. Let sentences connect. Keep them tight, not clipped.
- **Contrarian stays, delivered with a lighter touch.** The "say the uncomfortable thing" move works — but framed as a shared observation ("todos lo sabemos, pero nadie lo dice") rather than a blunt accusation.
- **What survives:** the claim leads, proof beats adjectives, and the Spanish filler-words are banned too (`potenciar`, `soluciones integrales`, `sin fisuras`, `en el mundo actual`).

**The test for any localized piece:** if you back-translate it, does it still lead with a claim, name a real pain, and prove instead of promise? If yes, the voice survived. If it went flat or generic, it's a translation, not a transcreation.
